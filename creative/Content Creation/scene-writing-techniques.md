## 1. CINEMATIC LANGUAGE AND SHOT TYPES

### 1.1 Basic Shot Composition
#### Shot Sizes and Framing
##### Wide and Establishing Shots
- Extreme Wide Shot (EWS): Context and scale, entire environment visible
- Wide Shot (WS): Full subject in environment, establishing location
- Medium Wide Shot: Subject prominent but environment still important
- Use for: Scene establishment, location transition, spatial relationships

##### Medium Shots
- Medium Shot (MS): Waist up, conversational distance
- Medium Close-Up (MCU): Chest up, intimate but not extreme
- Use for: Dialogue, subject focus with some environment
- Balance of subject and context

##### Close-Ups
- Close-Up (CU): Head and shoulders, emotional detail visible
- Extreme Close-Up (ECU): Eyes, hands, objects, intense detail
- Insert Shot: Specific object or detail, informational
- Use for: Emotion, detail, emphasis, transition

##### Specialized Shots
- Over-the-Shoulder (OTS): Conversation and interaction
- Point-of-View (POV): Subjective camera, audience as character
- Dutch Angle: Tilted horizon, disorientation or tension
- Aerial/Drone Shot: Top-down or elevated perspective

### 1.2 Camera Movement Techniques
#### Static Camera Movements
- Tripod/Static: Stability, formal, controlled composition
- Fixed Frame: Subjects move within static frame
- Locked-Off Shot: Precision for VFX or time-lapse
- Use for: Formal presentations, interviews, product shots

#### Dynamic Camera Movements
##### Pan and Tilt
- Pan: Horizontal rotation, follow action or reveal
- Tilt: Vertical rotation, reveal height or depth
- Whip Pan: Fast pan for transition or energy
- Speed and smoothness variations for mood

##### Dolly and Track
- Dolly In: Move toward subject, intensify or reveal
- Dolly Out: Move away from subject, reveal context or isolate
- Tracking Shot: Follow subject in motion, dynamic energy
- Arc/Curved Dolly: Circular movement around subject

##### Crane and Jib
- Crane Up: Vertical rise, opening up or conclusion
- Crane Down: Vertical descent, closing in or intimacy
- Combination Moves: Crane + pan/tilt for complex reveals

##### Handheld and Steadicam
- Handheld: Raw, intimate, documentary feel, controlled shake
- Steadicam: Smooth following, fluid through spaces
- Gimbal/Stabilizer: Floating camera, dreamlike quality
- Walk-and-Talk: Follow conversation in motion

### 1.3 Advanced Composition Principles
#### Rule of Thirds and Balance
- Subject placement on intersection points
- Horizon line on upper or lower third
- Leading room (space in direction of gaze/motion)
- Headroom (space above subject's head)
- Asymmetrical balance for visual interest

#### Depth and Layering
- Foreground, Middle Ground, Background separation
- Depth of field control (shallow vs deep focus)
- Leading lines to guide eye through frame
- Frame within frame for depth and focus
- Overlapping elements for dimensionality

## 3. SCENE WRITING FORMAT

### 3.1 Professional Script Format
#### Scene Heading (Slug Line)
```
INT. COFFEE SHOP - DAY
```
- INT (interior) or EXT (exterior)
- Location name in CAPS
- Time of day (DAY, NIGHT, MORNING, DUSK, etc.)

#### Action Lines
```
SARAH (28, confident marketing executive) strides into the bustling coffee shop.
She scans the room, her eyes landing on JAMES, who sits alone at a corner table,
nervously checking his phone.

The BARISTA calls out orders over the hiss of the espresso machine.
```
- Present tense, active voice
- Character names in CAPS on first mention
- Important sounds in CAPS
- Concise, visual, active descriptions
- What the camera sees, not internal thoughts

#### Camera Direction (use sparingly)
```
CLOSE ON Sarah's phone screen - a text from James: "We need to talk."

PULL BACK TO REVEAL her worried expression.
```
- Only when essential to story
- Smooth reading flow maintained
- Director may ignore, but suggests vision

### 3.2 Visual Description Techniques
#### Show, Don't Tell
**Weak**: "John is nervous."
**Strong**: "John's fingers drum the table. He checks his watch for the third time in a minute."

**Weak**: "The room is messy."
**Strong**: "Clothes spill from an overturned laundry basket. Pizza boxes stack three-high on the coffee table. Sunlight struggles through dust-coated blinds."

#### Sensory Detail Integration
- Visual: What we see (80% of description)
- Audio: What we hear (ambient sound, dialogue, music)
- Movement: Action and motion within frame
- Texture: Visual texture that implies touch
- Limited taste/smell (via visual metaphor)

#### Types of Montages
- Training montage: Time compression, skill development
- Relationship montage: Development over time
- Investigation montage: Information gathering and revelation
- Travel montage: Geographic movement and passage
- Transformation montage: Change and evolution

#### Montage Construction
- Clear beginning, middle, end within montage
- Progression and escalation visible
- Music/sound integration essential
- Transition techniques (cuts, dissolves, wipes)
- Emotional arc maintained
- Time passage indicated visually

### 4.2 Parallel Action and Cross-Cutting
- Multiple simultaneous storylines
- Building tension through intercutting
- Converging narratives
- Contrasting situations
- Time synchronization or asynchronization
- Thematic connections between scenes
- Rhythm and pacing through cutting

- Spatial relationships between characters
- Eye line and gaze direction

## Best Practices

1. **Write for the camera** - Describe only what can be filmed
2. **Use active voice** - Keep energy and momentum in prose
3. **Be specific but concise** - Vivid without verbosity
4. **Suggest, don't dictate** - Collaborate with director/DP
5. **Vary shot types** - Mix wide, medium, close for visual interest
6. **Motivate camera movement** - Every move has purpose
7. **Consider editing** - Write shots that will cut together
8. **Light for mood** - Lighting is as important as composition
9. **Show character through action** - Visual before dialogue
10. **Scout locations in mind** - Practical and achievable

## Tips for Success

- Watch films in your genre and analyze shot choices
- Create reference images/mood boards before writing
- Consider time of day for lighting continuity
- Write transitions between scenes in action lines
- Balance spectacle with storytelling (style serves story)
- Account for actors' blocking and movement
- Plan coverage for editing flexibility
- Use parentheticals sparingly in dialogue
- Describe sound and music in action lines
- Leave room for directorial interpretation and collaboration
