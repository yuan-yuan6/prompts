# Porter's Five Forces Analysis Framework

## Purpose
Analyze industry structure and competitive forces to understand profit potential and inform strategic positioning by examining rivalry, buyer power, supplier power, threat of substitutes, and threat of new entrants.

## Quick Start

**Want to assess industry attractiveness quickly?** Here's the minimal approach:

### When to Use This
- Evaluating whether to enter a new market or industry
- Understanding why industry profitability is high or low
- Identifying strategic positioning opportunities
- Planning competitive responses
- Assessing acquisition targets or partnership opportunities

### Minimal Example
```
Industry: Cloud Storage Services
Quick Assessment:
- Rivalry: HIGH (Dropbox, Google Drive, OneDrive, Box competing fiercely)
- Buyer Power: HIGH (low switching costs, many alternatives)
- Supplier Power: LOW (commodity data centers and bandwidth)
- Threat of Substitutes: MEDIUM (physical storage, alternative cloud solutions)
- Threat of New Entrants: MEDIUM (economies of scale needed, but low capital for MVP)

Overall Attractiveness: MEDIUM-LOW (high competition, powerful buyers)
Strategic Implication: Differentiate through specialization or enterprise features
```

### Basic 5-Step Workflow
1. **Define the Industry** - Specify exact industry boundaries and scope
2. **Analyze Each Force** - Systematically evaluate all five competitive forces
3. **Assess Force Strength** - Rate each force as HIGH, MEDIUM, or LOW
4. **Determine Overall Attractiveness** - Synthesize findings into industry profit potential
5. **Identify Strategic Implications** - Develop positioning strategy based on force dynamics

**Time to complete**: 2-4 hours for basic analysis, 1-2 weeks for comprehensive industry study

**Pro tip**: Focus most attention on the 1-2 strongest forces - they drive industry economics

## Usage Examples

### Example 1: Pharmaceutical Industry
**High Attractiveness Industry**

- **Rivalry**: MEDIUM (consolidation, patent-protected products differentiate)
- **Buyer Power**: MEDIUM (insurance companies negotiate, but patients less sensitive)
- **Supplier Power**: LOW (chemical inputs are commodities)
- **Substitutes**: LOW (few alternatives to proven drugs)
- **New Entrants**: LOW (FDA approval, high R&D costs, patent protection)

**Result**: Historically high profitability (15-20% ROIC)

---

### Example 2: U.S. Airline Industry
**Low Attractiveness Industry**

- **Rivalry**: HIGH (commoditized service, price competition, excess capacity)
- **Buyer Power**: HIGH (price-sensitive, low switching costs, transparent pricing)
- **Supplier Power**: HIGH (Boeing/Airbus duopoly, unionized labor, concentrated fuel suppliers)
- **Substitutes**: MEDIUM (cars for short routes, video conferencing)
- **New Entrants**: MEDIUM (deregulation lowered barriers, but high capital needs)

**Result**: Chronically low profitability (many bankruptcies)

---

### Example 3: Enterprise Software (SaaS)
**Moderately Attractive Industry**

- **Rivalry**: MEDIUM-HIGH (many players, but differentiation possible)
- **Buyer Power**: MEDIUM (high switching costs once implemented, but many alternatives)
- **Supplier Power**: LOW (cloud infrastructure is commodity, talent competitive but available)
- **Substitutes**: MEDIUM (on-premise software, custom development)
- **New Entrants**: MEDIUM (low initial capital, but scaling and customer acquisition expensive)

**Result**: Wide profitability variance (winners highly profitable, long tail struggles)

## Best Practices

### Do's ✅
- **Define Industry Precisely**: Narrow enough to be meaningful, broad enough to capture substitutes
- **Use Quantitative Data**: Percentages, market shares, concentration ratios
- **Consider Force Interactions**: Forces affect each other (e.g., high rivalry often means buyers have power)
- **Look at Trends**: How are forces changing over time?
- **Be Objective**: Don't let wishful thinking bias the analysis
- **Focus on Structure, Not Outcomes**: Analyze underlying forces, not just current profitability
- **Update Regularly**: Industry structure evolves; reanalyze every 2-3 years

### Don'ts ❌
- **Don't Confuse Industry with Segment**: Be clear about boundaries
- **Don't Ignore Weak Forces**: Even low-strength forces matter if changing rapidly
- **Don't Stop at Analysis**: Translate into strategic action
- **Don't Treat as Static**: Industries evolve; anticipate structural changes
- **Don't Oversimplify**: Some situations require nuanced HIGH-MEDIUM or MEDIUM-LOW ratings
- **Don't Ignore Complements**: Sixth force in some industries (e.g., apps for smartphones)

## Related Frameworks

- **Blue Ocean Strategy**: Escape unattractive industry structure by creating new market space
- **PESTEL Analysis**: Macro forces that shape industry structure
- **Value Chain Analysis**: How to position within industry to defend against forces
- **Strategic Group Analysis**: Identify mobility barriers within industry
- **Resource-Based View**: Internal capabilities to defend against external forces

## Success Metrics

- **Accuracy**: Does analysis predict industry profitability? (Validate with actual ROIC data)
- **Actionability**: Does analysis lead to clear strategic choices?
- **Consistency**: Do independent analysts reach similar conclusions?
- **Predictive Value**: Does it anticipate structural changes before they occur?
